Core Thesis

Geospatial evidence for where microhousing demand and fit are likely to concentrate.

The research argument on this page follows the map sequence chronologically and uses it to identify suitable sites for user testing and market research in Metro Manila. It begins by establishing the study area, then narrows attention through population density, transportation infrastructure, healthcare access, and land-use characteristics. Those layers are combined into a composite accessibility view and then checked against the geographic concentration of competitor listings. Project abstract

Identifying Suitable Sites for User Testing and Market Research

This study identifies optimal locations for conducting user testing of a microhousing rental platform and surveys of competing platforms in Metro Manila, Philippines. It integrates multiple geospatial datasets, including transportation infrastructure, healthcare access, population density, and land-use characteristics, to conduct a composite analysis. Transportation accessibility is assessed using buffer analyses around bus stations with a 150-meter radius and train stations with a 500-meter radius. Because jeepney stops are much more densely distributed, accessibility for that mode is evaluated through kernel density estimation. Healthcare accessibility is incorporated through a 500-meter buffer. These layers are then intersected to create a composite feature layer representing general accessibility to essential urban services. The resulting accessibility layer is analyzed alongside barangay-level population density data and polygons identifying business and university districts. The working hypothesis is that areas with the greatest overlap among these factors are the most suitable locations for user testing and survey implementation. The study further evaluates Binondo, Manila's historic Chinatown, as a potentially strong user testing site through spatial comparison with other business districts and by examining the concentration of listings on competing microhousing rental platforms.

Working rationale

  • The study asks a user-testing and market-research question, not just a mapping question: where should BahayTrust go first to observe renter behavior and benchmark competing platforms?
  • Population density helps establish where demand is most likely to be structurally concentrated across barangays and districts.
  • Transportation and healthcare accessibility narrow that field to places where compact urban living is more practical on a day-to-day basis.
  • The composite and competitor layers together convert a broad housing thesis into a more defensible list of candidate testing corridors and districts.

Chronology of the argument

Frames 1-3 establish geography and population concentration. Frames 4-5 introduce transportation and healthcare accessibility. Frame 6 integrates those signals into a composite suitability layer. Frames 7-8 then test whether competitor listing concentration corroborates the same urban corridors, including the possibility that Binondo functions as a particularly strong business-district candidate.

Map Frames

Chronological map sequence supporting the user-testing hypothesis.

Read the frames in order. Each one adds one more layer to the argument, moving from simple geographic orientation to a more specific claim about where BahayTrust should conduct early user testing in Metro Manila.

1. Base geography

The first frame establishes the overall study geography across Metro Manila and nearby cities. It defines the physical area in which later density, accessibility, and competitor layers will be compared.

This matters because the research is not evaluating a single neighborhood in isolation. It is comparing central Manila, adjacent western districts, northern corridors, and eastern extensions inside one metropolitan system.

In narrative terms, this frame sets the board. Every claim that follows about density, access, and listing concentration is meaningful only because it can be located within this broader regional footprint.

2. Population density

The second frame asks where population concentration is strongest at a high level. It helps identify the portions of the metro where compact housing demand is most likely to be structurally durable.

The visual pattern suggests that the strongest concentrations are not distributed evenly. Instead, there is a more intense urban weighting toward the western and central parts of the study area, where dense residential activity is already more embedded in the city fabric.

For BahayTrust, this frame introduces the first serious screening logic: if microhousing demand is going to be tested efficiently, the first pass should start where population pressure is already most visible.

3. Density by district

The third frame sharpens that density logic at the district or barangay level. This is where the analysis starts moving from broad metro intuition toward specific submarket prioritization.

Instead of simply concluding that Manila is dense, this layer helps identify which districts inside the metro are more tightly packed and therefore more relevant for street-level testing, survey distribution, or early supply discovery.

The shift from frame 2 to frame 3 is important analytically: the argument moves from regional demand potential to more actionable district selection. This is where launch sequencing starts becoming concrete.

4. Transportation accessibility

The fourth frame introduces transportation accessibility using bus and train buffers, plus jeepney density. This reframes the problem from where people are concentrated to where dense living still works for everyday movement.

In a microhousing context, density without movement can create friction rather than opportunity. Residents in smaller units rely heavily on surrounding urban access, especially for commuting, errands, and daily circulation.

This frame therefore acts as a livability filter. It narrows the set of promising districts to those where transportation infrastructure supports the practical reality of compact urban life.

5. Hospital accessibility

The fifth frame adds hospital proximity as a second everyday service layer. Together with transport, it helps distinguish dense but under-supported areas from stronger urban living environments.

Hospital access is not the only service variable that matters, but it is a meaningful proxy for broader urban support. Areas with better service proximity are more likely to feel viable for renters assessing whether smaller living arrangements are acceptable.

By this point in the sequence, the research has already moved beyond pure density. The emerging question is not just where people are, but where people can reasonably live compactly while maintaining basic access to the city.

6. Urban accessibility composite

The sixth frame is where the argument becomes operational. By intersecting accessibility layers with density and designated districts, it produces a more decision-ready suitability view for early user testing.

This is the most synthetic frame in the sequence because it combines multiple dimensions into one spatial judgment. It is no longer just showing inputs; it is suggesting candidate zones where those inputs overlap strongly enough to justify direct testing.

For BahayTrust, this is the transition from research evidence to operational prioritization. It is the frame most directly tied to where the team should spend time, recruit participants, and evaluate early product-market fit.

7. Competitor listings

The seventh frame checks the suitability hypothesis against actual competitor listing points. If listing behavior clusters in the same urban bands, that supports the case for testing in those areas rather than relying on theory alone.

This matters because suitability models can otherwise remain abstract. Competitor listings introduce observed market behavior, offering a concrete indication that users and suppliers are already interacting in particular districts.

The value of this frame is corroboration. It asks whether the theoretical corridor identified through density and access is also visible in the real geography of competing platform activity.

8. Competitor listing density

The eighth frame converts those points into concentration. It is the clearest final corroboration layer, because it shows whether competitor activity intensifies in the same districts identified by density and accessibility analysis, including the Binondo-centered corridor discussed in the abstract.

Density heat helps move beyond scattered points and makes the competitive landscape legible at a glance. Clusters become far easier to compare with the earlier accessibility and density layers.

As the final frame in the sequence, this one closes the argument: if listing concentration reinforces the same urban corridors highlighted by the composite suitability layer, then the proposed user-testing sites are not only theoretically plausible but also market-facing in practice.
